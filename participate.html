pARTicipate


Encouraging community involvement to deepen the impact of public art in Pittsburgh

Taxpayers fund public art - to what end? What is the role public art plays in communities, and how might we deepen its impact? The city of Pittsburgh, in collaboration with Carnegie Mellon’s SmartCities Institute, funded a project to understand how public art contributes to the livability of a city. Our team used a suite of user research and needs validation methodologies to explore how public art might strengthen communities.

Interviewing local artists about their hopes for art in a community.

Our team used synthesis and reframing techniques throughout research.

We created a single affinity diagram to draw insights from the first phase of research.

We crafted a low-fidelity experiential "pretotype" to cheaply validate user needs.



PHASE 1: EXPLORATION

Based on our contextual inquiry and interviews with local residents, we learned that citizens of Pittsburgh value art deeply. However, when actually asked to interact with public art, they expressed a disconnect between how they felt and the impact they believe art could have on a community.


“The local art community is the soul of any city or town.”

“You want to see art that reflects you and challenges you - it should say something that reverberates.”

"Art is documentation - it's telling stories of people who don't have [their own] platform."

Throughout these interviews, users emphasized that they valued learning more about the art, artist, and the context in which it was made.

To observe realtime interactions with public art, we performed think-aloud interviews with residents using artarchive.org. While responding to pieces, participants highlighted their interest in art when it helped them engage with a community or learn about history, like Pittsburgh's industrial heritage.


PHASE 2: RESEARCH THROUGH VALIDATION

With these initial insights, we generated several storyboards exploring how art could better engage communities. We tested our assumptions and explored user motivations in speed dating interviews around:


1. The potential for art to educate citizens about changes in a community over time
2. The weight of authenticity and locality to art's value
3. Individual dialogue and involvement with the art creation process
4. The value of direct interaction with artists
5. The desire for deeper relationships in a community around art


While users rejected ideas around deeper engagement with art or artists in-person, they were excited by the idea of having a small say in art for their neighborhood. Our team hypothesized that we could positively influence the value of public art to local communities by soliciting their input in its creation. To further validate this idea, we crafted a pretotype of a public art survey and tested it on Pittsburgh residents at a local coffee shop.

Experience Prototype Goals

1. Do individuals value contributing to public art development in their communities?
2. Does participating in public art development help individuals feel more connected to their communities?
3. Does individuals’ interest in participating extend beyond the initial experience?

We built a low-fidelity prototype of an interactive art survey to perform with community members. During the activity, participants were asked to rank themes they would like to see reflected in public art in their community, as well as their preferred forms of art. The activity confirmed our hypotheses: though participants rated their connection to their community relatively highly pre-activity (mean 3.7/5), their post-activity report increased an average of 0.2 points. If the art was created, participants were very interested in following its progress and reveal. When selecting types of art, participants thought about the needs of their entire community.


“[After performing the activity] my community felt bigger”

“This changed how I view my role [in the community]”

"We have a lot of murals - I wanted to pick something we don’t have that would be used by a lot of people"
